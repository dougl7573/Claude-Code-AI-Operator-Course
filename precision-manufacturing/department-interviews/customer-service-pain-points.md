# Customer Service Interview - David Thompson

**Date:** Week 3 of your new role
**Department:** Customer Service
**Team Lead:** David Thompson
**Team Size:** 6 people (David + 5 reps)

---

## The Conversation

You found David in the customer service area - an open office space with 6 desks. Phones ringing constantly.

**You:** "David! Thanks for meeting. Sarah said you might have some workflow issues I could help with?"

**David:** (finishing an email) "Workflow issues? Ha. More like Groundhog Day. Same questions, different day, forever. Grab a seat."

*He closes his laptop lid to focus*

**David:** "So here's the deal. We handle customer support for 800+ active accounts. Phone, email, chat - the whole nine yards.

About 60% of our support volume is the SAME TEN QUESTIONS over and over:

1. How do I track my order?
2. What's your return policy?
3. Can I get a copy of my invoice?
4. How do I update my billing information?
5. What are your lead times for custom orders?
6. Do you ship internationally?
7. What's your warranty policy?
8. How do I set up a new user in your portal?
9. Can I get a quote for bulk ordering?
10. Where's my order confirmation email?

**You:** "And you're answering these manually every time?"

**David:** "YUP. Let me show you..."

*He opens his email. 47 unread messages.*

**David:** "Watch this."

*He opens an email: "Hi, what's your return policy?"*

*He opens a Word document titled "Email Templates - Customer Service"*

*Scrolls to "Return Policy Template"*

*Copies the text*

*Pastes into email reply*

*Makes a few edits: "Hi Sarah" → adds customer name*

*Sends*

**David:** "That's the process. For EVERY. SINGLE. RESPONSE.

My team probably sends 200 emails a day. Maybe 120 of them are literally copy-paste from that Word doc.

But here's the problem: It's not truly copy-paste. We have to customize:
- Customer name
- Order number (if relevant)
- Product name (if relevant)
- Dates (if relevant)
- Links to their specific invoice/tracking/account

So each response takes 2-4 minutes.

Times 120 emails per day.

That's... 240-480 minutes. Or 4-8 HOURS per day of template-based responses."

**You:** "That's a lot."

**David:** "And that's just email. We get phone calls too - same questions. But at least on the phone we can just SAY the answer. Email requires typing it out every time."

---

## The Pain Points

**You:** "What's the biggest issue with this?"

**David:** "Let me count the ways:

**1. It's Brutally Repetitive**
My team hates it. I hired these people because they're good at problem-solving and customer relationships. Instead, they spend half their day copy-pasting templates.

Morale is LOW. Two people have quit in the last 6 months. Exit interviews both mentioned "boring, repetitive work."

**2. Inconsistent Responses**
Six people, one Word doc. But everyone edits it differently:
- Jessica is super friendly, adds lots of exclamation points
- Marcus is concise and formal
- Sarah uses emojis (customers love it, but it's not professional)

So customers get wildly different experiences depending on who answers.

**3. Templates Get Outdated**
Our return policy changed in September. Did everyone update their template? Who knows!

I found out two weeks later that Marcus was still sending the OLD policy because his Word doc was cached locally.

**4. Slow Response Times**
Even with templates, we're averaging 4-6 hour response times on email. Customers expect faster.

If someone could just... instantly get the answer to "What's your return policy?" instead of waiting for us to copy-paste? Way better experience.

**5. No Data/Learning**
We have no idea which questions are most common, which templates are used most, which answers lead to escalations. It's all invisible.

If I knew that "lead time questions" were 30% of volume, I'd put that answer on our website homepage. But I don't have data."

---

## What They've Tried

**You:** "Has anyone tried to improve this?"

**David:** "Oh yeah. Multiple attempts.

**Attempt 1: Shared Email Templates (Gmail)**
We use Gmail. I set up "Canned Responses" (Gmail's template feature).

Problems:
- Limited customization (can't easily insert customer name, order number, etc.)
- Still manual - you have to remember the right template
- Doesn't work on mobile (reps answer emails on phones sometimes)
- No version control - if I update a template, nobody knows

We used it for a month. Reps said it was MORE annoying than Word doc copy-paste.

**Attempt 2: Knowledge Base (We tried Zendesk)**
I convinced Sarah to trial Zendesk for 3 months.

The plan was:
- Build a help center with articles
- Customers find their own answers
- Reduce support volume

Results:
- Cost $89/month for our team size
- I spent 40 hours writing articles
- Customers didn't use it (only 12% of traffic went there)
- We still got the same volume of emails

Canceled after the trial. Waste of time and money.

**Attempt 3: Macros (Text Expander)**
Marcus found a Chrome extension that does text expansion. Type ";;return" and it expands to the full return policy template.

Problems:
- Cost $5/month per user ($30/month total)
- Only works in browser, not phone/other apps
- Still doesn't insert customer-specific details
- Have to remember all the shortcodes

Three people use it. The others forget the shortcuts.

**Current State:** Back to the Word doc. It's clunky, but everyone understands it."

---

## What Success Would Look Like

**You:** "If you could wave a magic wand, what would the ideal solution be?"

**David:** "Okay, dream scenario:

**Option A: Smart Email Assistant (For My Team)**
1. Rep gets an email: "What's your return policy?"
2. System recognizes this is a common question
3. Suggests a draft response with:
   - Proper greeting using customer's name
   - Return policy answer
   - Relevant personalization (order number if applicable)
   - Professional signature
4. Rep reviews it, maybe tweaks one sentence, sends
5. Time per email: 30 seconds instead of 3 minutes

**Option B: Customer Self-Service (Dream Bigger)**
1. Customer emails: "What's your return policy?"
2. System auto-replies instantly with accurate answer
3. If customer replies back (wasn't satisfied), it goes to a human
4. But 70% of simple questions get answered immediately

**What Either Option Gives Us:**
- Faster response times (minutes instead of hours)
- Consistent answers (everyone gets the same accurate info)
- More time for complex issues (the stuff that actually needs human judgment)
- Better customer experience
- Better employee morale (less boring work)

**You:** "Which one do you think is more realistic?"

**David:** "Honestly? Option A feels safer.

Option B is cool but... I'm nervous about auto-replying to customers without human review. What if the AI gets something wrong? What if it sounds too robotic?

Start with Option A - help my team write better emails faster. If that works? Then we can explore auto-replies.

Baby steps."
